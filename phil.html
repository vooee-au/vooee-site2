Website & GoHighLevel

What it is, what to fix, and the migration question

Your websites (philvoysey.com and possibilityofus.com) are built on GoHighLevel, a powerful marketing platform that appears to have been set up from a template and not yet fully finished. That's not unusual. But it does mean there are some things worth cleaning up before you invest energy in driving more people there.

What GoHighLevel actually is

GoHighLevel is an all-in-one marketing platform. It handles your website, contact database (CRM), email marketing, booking, forms, and automations all in one place. Think of it as the engine room of your business's online presence. It's genuinely capable, and having a VA who can work in it is an advantage.

The challenge is that GoHighLevel was built for marketing agencies, not sole traders. So it has a lot of features you'll never need, the templates come loaded with placeholder copy, and the links and automations behind forms often don't get set up properly out of the box. That's almost certainly what's creating the dead links and generic text you're seeing.

Audit checklist, go through this with your VA

Share this page with your VA directly. These are the things most likely to be causing problems right now.

Fix now

Dead and broken links

Click every button and link on every page, on both sites. Anything that goes to a 404, an empty page, or loops back on itself, note it and either fix it or remove it. Dead links lose people immediately and signal that the site isn't looked after.

Placeholder and template text

Any copy that sounds generic or clearly left over from a template needs to go. For example, on the Resources tab of the Possibility of Us website right now, the copy reads: "Your Content Here Your Paragraph text goes Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipisicing elit. Autem dolore, alias, numquam enim ab voluptate id quam harum ducimus cupiditate similique quisquam et deserunt, recusandae." That's visible to anyone who visits the site, and it signals that the page isn't finished. Replace it with your words, or remove it entirely. Even a simpler page with your own voice is better than a full page of borrowed marketing language.

The happiness survey and what happens next

It looks like the happiness survey button now takes visitors to a contact form rather than the original Dropbox link. That's a step forward, but it raises a question worth answering clearly: what happens after someone fills in that contact form? Do they get an email? A phone call? A booking link? Nothing? Walk through the flow yourself as if you were a first-time visitor. If there's no clear next step after they hit submit, that's where people will quietly drop off. Make sure there's a response, even a simple confirmation with a next action, so the person knows their interest landed somewhere real.

Review soon

Your contact list, who's actually in there?

GoHighLevel stores all your contacts in a CRM. Check who's in there and whether they're organised in any way. Even a rough list of "people who've shown interest" is worth having, it's the foundation of any email or follow-up strategy you might build later.

Are any automations running?

GHL has a powerful automation builder. Check what's been set up, if anything. Templates often include automation "shells" that were never switched on. Nothing needs to be running right now, but it's worth knowing what exists so you can decide what to activate.

When ready

Basic website analytics

Do you know how many people visit your sites each month? If not, it's worth setting up, GoHighLevel supports Google Analytics. You can't improve what you can't measure, and traffic numbers will tell you a lot about where to focus.

Discoverability: getting found on Google and by AI

There are two ways people find businesses online now, and both matter for you.

SEO (Search Engine Optimisation) is about making sure Google can find your site and shows it when someone searches for something relevant. Think of it as the shop sign and the listing in the phone book. Right now, philvoysey.com shows up when someone searches your name directly, but possibilityofus.com is much harder to find. If someone types "life coaching for men Blue Mountains" or "midlife coaching Australia," your site doesn't come up. That's because the pages are likely missing metatags (the behind-the-scenes labels that tell Google what the page is about), the page titles may be generic, and there may not be enough real, original text on each page for Google to index properly.

AEO (AI Engine Optimisation) is the newer version of the same idea. When someone asks ChatGPT, Claude, or Google's AI overview a question like "Where can I find coaching for men going through a midlife transition in Australia?", the AI pulls from websites it can understand clearly. The same things that help with Google also help here: clear page titles, descriptive headings, real content written in your own words, and structured information about what you do, where you're based, and who you help.

The good news is that fixing one fixes both. The practical step: speak with whoever helped set up your GoHighLevel site and ask them to review and update the metatags (page titles, descriptions, and heading structure) across both sites. If they're not sure how, this is something I can help with too.

The migration question

We talked through this on our call and agreed: migration isn't the right move right now. The effort required to rebuild your pages, re-import contacts, reconnect your domain, and get your VA up to speed on a new system doesn't outweigh what you'd gain. GHL isn't the problem. The current setup just isn't finished yet.

That said, the tools in this space are improving quickly, and migration will likely get easier over time. Worth revisiting in 6 to 12 months once the foundation is solid. For now, a focused cleanup is the right call, and it costs a fraction of the time and money a migration would.

Short-term effort, long-term payoff

A working website with a real lead capture tool is the difference between word-of-mouth being your only channel and having something working quietly in the background while you're out coaching. It's worth getting right once.

A note on token limits

Even on the paid plan, Claude has limits on how much it can process in one conversation. Think of it like a whiteboard that has a fixed amount of space. If a conversation gets very long, Claude starts to "forget" the earlier parts. The practical workaround: start a fresh conversation for each new task rather than letting one conversation run for days. Keep tasks focused.
